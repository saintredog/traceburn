# 1.4 — Legal & Compliance Review

**TraceBurn Phase 1 Research**
**Date:** 2026-04-01

This document covers the legal frameworks governing personal data removal, what is enforceable vs. best-effort, scraping legality, and required disclaimers for TraceBurn output reports.

## 1. CCPA — California Consumer Privacy Act

### Overview
The CCPA (effective January 1, 2020), enhanced by CPRA (California Privacy Rights Act, effective January 1, 2023), grants California residents specific rights over their personal data held by covered businesses.

### Consumer Rights Under CCPA/CPRA

| Right | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Right to Know** | Request disclosure of what personal info a business has collected, used, shared, or sold about you (categories and specific pieces) |
| **Right to Delete** | Request deletion of personal info the business has collected about you (subject to exceptions) |
| **Right to Opt-Out of Sale/Sharing** | Direct a business to stop selling or sharing your personal info (the "Do Not Sell or Share My Personal Information" link) |
| **Right to Correct** | Request correction of inaccurate personal information |
| **Right to Limit Use of Sensitive PI** | Restrict use of sensitive personal information (SSN, financial, health, precise geolocation, etc.) to necessary purposes |
| **Right to Non-Discrimination** | Cannot be denied service or penalized for exercising CCPA rights |

### Which Businesses Are Covered

A for-profit business operating in California is subject to CCPA if it meets **any one** of the following:

1. **Annual gross revenue > $25 million** (worldwide)
2. **Buys, sells, receives, or shares personal info of 100,000+ consumers or households per year**
3. **Derives 50%+ of annual revenue from selling consumers' personal information**

**Practical implication for TraceBurn:** Most major data brokers (Spokeo, Whitepages, BeenVerified, Intelius, LexisNexis, etc.) meet threshold #2 and are covered. Smaller niche brokers may not be covered if they are not for-profit or operate entirely outside California.

### How to Submit a CCPA Deletion Request

1. **Locate the privacy contact.** Covered businesses must provide at minimum: a toll-free phone number, a web form, or an email address. They must designate a privacy contact or DPO.
2. **Verify your identity.** Business may require identity verification (name, email, address matching their records, or a signed declaration).
3. **Submit the request.** State: "Pursuant to the California Consumer Privacy Act (Cal. Civ. Code § 1798.105), I request deletion of all personal information you have collected, maintained, or shared about me."
4. **Receive confirmation.** Business must confirm receipt within **10 business days** and complete deletion within **45 calendar days** (with one 45-day extension permitted if they notify you).
5. **Follow up on third parties.** Business must direct service providers and contractors to delete your data as well.

### Exceptions to the Right to Delete

Businesses may refuse deletion if the data is necessary to:
- Complete a transaction the consumer requested
- Detect/prevent security incidents or fraud
- Comply with a legal obligation
- Exercise free speech or another legal right
- Research in the public interest

**Data brokers frequently use the "legal obligation" and "public records" exceptions.** Challenge these if the data is clearly commercial in nature.

### Enforcement

| Body | Role |
|------|------|
| **California Attorney General (AG)** | Enforcement authority; can bring civil penalties up to $2,500/violation (unintentional) or $7,500/violation (intentional) |
| **California Privacy Protection Agency (CPPA)** | Dedicated agency created by CPRA; can issue regulations, conduct audits, and bring enforcement actions independent of AG |
| **Private right of action** | Limited: consumers can sue for data breaches involving certain unencrypted data ($100–$750 per consumer per incident) |

### Response Timeline Summary

| Milestone | Deadline |
|-----------|----------|
| Acknowledge receipt | 10 business days |
| Complete deletion | 45 calendar days |
| Extension (if needed) | Additional 45 days (with notice) |
| Total maximum | 90 calendar days |

## 2. GDPR — Right to Erasure (Article 17)

### Overview
The General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), effective May 25, 2018, applies across the European Union. Article 17 grants individuals the "right to erasure" (also called the "right to be forgotten").

### Who GDPR Applies To

GDPR applies based on **where the data subject is located** and **where the controller operates**, not citizenship:

- **EU residents** can invoke GDPR against any organization that processes their data, regardless of where that organization is located.
- **Any organization established in the EU** must comply with GDPR regardless of where the data subject is located.
- **Non-EU organizations** must comply if they offer goods/services to EU residents or monitor the behavior of EU residents.

**For US-based TraceBurn users:** GDPR applies if:
- The user is physically in the EU, OR
- The data is held by an EU-based company or a company with EU operations subject to GDPR

**Practical note:** Many large data brokers (Google, Facebook, LinkedIn, etc.) have EU operations and maintain GDPR compliance processes globally. A US resident requesting deletion from these companies can often use the GDPR process even if CCPA doesn't apply to their specific situation.

### When Article 17 Applies

The right to erasure applies when **any one** of the following conditions are met:

1. The data is no longer necessary for the purpose for which it was collected
2. The data subject withdraws consent (and there is no other legal basis)
3. The data subject objects under Article 21 and there are no overriding legitimate grounds
4. The data was unlawfully processed
5. Erasure is required to comply with EU law
6. The data subject is a child and consent was given in relation to information society services

### When Businesses Can Refuse

Controllers may refuse if the data is necessary for:
- Freedom of expression and information
- Compliance with a legal obligation
- Public health in the public interest (Articles 9(2)(h)/(i))
- Archiving/research/statistical purposes in the public interest
- Establishment, exercise, or defense of legal claims

### How to Submit a GDPR Erasure Request

1. **Submit in writing** to the data controller's Data Protection Officer (DPO) or privacy contact (most large companies have an online form at privacy.company.com or gdpr.company.com).
2. **Identify yourself** and specify what data you want erased.
3. **State the legal basis** (usually "the data is no longer necessary for the purpose it was collected" or "I withdraw consent").
4. **Controller must respond within 30 days** (extendable by 2 additional months for complex cases, with notice).
5. **Right to complain:** If the controller refuses or fails to respond, you can lodge a complaint with your national supervisory authority (e.g., ICO in UK, CNIL in France, BfDI in Germany).

### Template GDPR Erasure Request Letter

```
Subject: Right to Erasure Request — Article 17 GDPR

To the Data Protection Officer / Privacy Team at [Company Name],

I am writing to exercise my right to erasure under Article 17 of the General
Data Protection Regulation (GDPR 2016/679).

Please erase all personal data you hold about me, including but not limited
to: [full name / email address / physical address / phone number / any other
identifiers you hold].

I request erasure on the following grounds:
[ ] The data is no longer necessary for the purpose for which it was collected.
[ ] I withdraw the consent upon which processing was based.
[ ] I object to the processing under Article 21 GDPR.
[ ] The data has been unlawfully processed.

Please confirm in writing:
1. That you have erased my personal data, and
2. That you have notified any third parties to whom my data was disclosed.

If you require further information to identify me in your systems, please
contact me at [email address]. Please do not use any other data you hold
about me for any purpose other than to process this request.

I expect a response within 30 days as required by Article 12 GDPR.

Full name: [Name]
Date of birth: [DOB, if needed for identification]
Email: [Email]
Address: [Address, if needed for identification]

Date: [Date]
Signature: [Signature]
```

## 3. Legal Enforceability by Broker Type

### Enforceability Table

| Broker / Entity | Legal Basis for Removal | Enforceable? | Typical Outcome |
|-----------------|------------------------|--------------|-----------------|
| **Spokeo** | CCPA (covered); voluntary opt-out form | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 7–14 days via opt-out form; profile may reappear after 6–12 months |
| **Whitepages** | CCPA (covered); voluntary opt-out | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 24–72 hours; phone verification required |
| **BeenVerified** | CCPA (covered); opt-out form | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 24 hours via opt-out form |
| **Intelius** | CCPA (covered); opt-out form | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 72 hours |
| **MyLife** | CCPA (covered); opt-out + potential lawsuit history | Yes (CCPA; FTC enforcement precedent) | Removal within 5 days via opt-out form; historically non-compliant — escalate if unresponsive |
| **PeopleFinder** | CCPA (covered) | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 7 days |
| **TruthFinder** | CCPA (covered); opt-out form | Yes (CCPA) | Removal within 5 business days |
| **Radaris** | CCPA (partially); voluntary opt-out | Weak | Removal inconsistent; profile often re-aggregates from other sources |
| **LexisNexis** | CCPA (covered for consumer data); DPPA for driver/voter records | Partially (varies by data type) | Opt-out available for marketing; suppression files available; core records not removable |
| **Acxiom** | CCPA (covered); Acxiom opt-out portal | Yes (CCPA) | Removal via AboutTheData.com within 30 days |
| **Google (Search index)** | GDPR (Article 17) for EU residents; voluntary removal tool for others | Yes (GDPR for EU); Best-effort (US) | Google removes specific URLs; no legal obligation for non-EU users |
| **Facebook** | GDPR (EU users); CCPA (CA users); platform ToS for others | Yes for EU/CA; Best-effort globally | Account deletion removes data in 90 days; some data retained per legal obligation |
| **LinkedIn** | GDPR (EU); CCPA (CA) | Yes for EU/CA | Profile deletion removes public data within 7 days |
| **Paste sites (Pastebin, etc.)** | DMCA (for copyrighted content only); no general legal basis | Weak | Voluntary removal only; usually no obligation |
| **Breach databases (HIBP, Dehashed)** | None — breach data is already public | **Not enforceable** | Mitigation only; HIBP will mark an email as "sensitized" (hidden from public) upon request for sensitive cases |
| **Court records / public records** | State law varies; some states allow expungement | Varies by jurisdiction | Expungement possible for eligible cases; public records sites may not honor removal |
| **Social Security / government records** | Federal agency FOIA/Privacy Act rights | Limited | Agency processes only; no commercial removal |

### Key Enforceability Principles

1. **CCPA is the strongest tool for US residents** against data brokers that meet the coverage thresholds.
2. **GDPR is the strongest tool for EU residents** and applies to most large tech companies globally.
3. **Breach data is not removable** — only mitigatable.
4. **Public records** (court filings, property records, voter rolls) are legally accessible and generally cannot be removed from aggregator sites.
5. **Re-aggregation is the primary failure mode** — data removed from one broker often reappears within 6–12 months as brokers re-scrape public records and share data among themselves.

## 4. ToS and Scraping Legal Notes

### hiQ Labs v. LinkedIn (Key Case)

**Case:** hiQ Labs, Inc. v. LinkedIn Corp., 9th Circuit (2022)

**Core holding:** The Ninth Circuit held that scraping **publicly accessible** data from a website does not violate the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA), even if the site operator objects. The CFAA's prohibition on "unauthorized access" requires that the data be behind some form of access control (login, authentication, etc.).

**What this means for TraceBurn:**
- Scraping publicly available data (broker profiles viewable without logging in, Google search results, public social media posts) is generally legal under CFAA.
- This is the prevailing precedent in the 9th Circuit as of 2026. Other circuits have not uniformly adopted this view.
- **Important limitation:** This ruling addresses the CFAA specifically. It does not preempt state computer crime laws, ToS breach claims, or other causes of action.

### Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) Limits

The CFAA (18 U.S.C. § 1030) prohibits:
- Accessing a computer **without authorization** or **exceeding authorized access**
- Intentionally damaging a protected computer
- Trafficking in passwords

**CFAA limits for TraceBurn:**
- **Do NOT bypass authentication.** Any data behind a login wall (even if previously accessible) constitutes "without authorization" under CFAA. This includes: logging into broker accounts with credentials that are not the user's own, bypassing paywalls, or circumventing CAPTCHA with automation.
- **Do NOT use credentials obtained from breaches** to access systems — even to verify breach data.
- Rate limiting violations alone do not constitute a CFAA violation post-hiQ, but aggressive scraping can lead to IP blocks and civil claims under ToS.

### robots.txt Compliance

`robots.txt` is a voluntary protocol — it is not legally binding. However:
- Ignoring `robots.txt` is used as evidence of bad faith in civil litigation.
- Some ToS agreements explicitly incorporate `robots.txt` compliance, making violation a breach of contract.
- **TraceBurn policy:** Respect `robots.txt` directives for all automated requests. If `robots.txt` disallows a path, document the restriction and fall back to manual lookup guidance.

### Acceptable Use Rules for TraceBurn

**TraceBurn MUST:**
- Respect `robots.txt` directives
- Use rate limiting (minimum 1–2 seconds between requests per domain)
- Identify itself with a descriptive User-Agent string (e.g., `TraceBurn/1.0 (personal data scanner; contact@traceburn.app)`)
- Only access data that is publicly available without authentication
- Use official opt-out mechanisms (not scraping) for removal submissions where available
- Store only the minimum data needed to complete the scan (data minimization)

**TraceBurn MUST NOT:**
- Access gated or authenticated data (data behind a login)
- Use credentials obtained from breaches or third parties to access systems
- Submit requests at a rate that constitutes a denial-of-service attack
- Bypass CAPTCHA, Cloudflare challenges, or other bot-detection mechanisms programmatically
- Store or log raw passwords or password hashes retrieved from Dehashed or similar services
- Re-sell or redistribute personal data obtained during scans
- Automate the creation of accounts on data broker sites to access additional data

### Terms of Service Considerations

Most data broker sites explicitly prohibit automated scraping in their ToS. While ToS violations are civil (not criminal), they:
- Can result in IP bans and access termination
- Are occasionally used as a basis for cease-and-desist letters
- Can undermine TraceBurn's credibility if pursued aggressively

**Preferred approach:** Use official opt-out APIs and forms where available (e.g., Spokeo's opt-out endpoint, Acxiom's AboutTheData portal). Reserve scraping for discovery (finding profiles) and use form submission for removal actions.

## 5. Recommended Disclaimers for TraceBurn Output Reports

The following disclaimers should be included in all TraceBurn scan reports and the application's Terms of Service.

### Report Header Disclaimer

```
DISCLAIMER — TraceBurn Personal Data Exposure Report

This report is generated for your personal use only. It contains information
about where your personal data may appear online and provides guidance on
requesting removal. TraceBurn does not guarantee that removal requests will
be honored, that all sources of exposure have been identified, or that
identified data will be permanently removed.

This report does not constitute legal advice. For advice specific to your
situation, consult a qualified attorney.
```

### Accuracy Disclaimer

```
DATA ACCURACY: TraceBurn scans publicly available sources at the time of
the scan. Results reflect a snapshot and may not capture all exposure points.
New exposures may occur after the scan date. Re-scanning periodically is
recommended.
```

### Removal Outcomes Disclaimer

```
REMOVAL OUTCOMES: Submitting a removal request does not guarantee removal.
Data brokers may refuse requests based on exceptions in CCPA, GDPR, or their
own policies. Removed data may reappear as brokers re-aggregate information
from public records. TraceBurn is not responsible for the actions or
inactions of third-party data brokers.
```

### Breach Data Disclaimer

```
BREACH DATA: Information found in breach databases cannot be removed from
those databases or from copies that may have proliferated across the internet.
TraceBurn recommends changing passwords and enabling two-factor
authentication for any accounts identified in breaches. TraceBurn does not
store, display, or transmit passwords or password hashes found in breach data.
```

### Legal Jurisdiction Disclaimer

```
LEGAL RIGHTS: The legal rights available to you depend on your jurisdiction
and the jurisdiction of the data controller. Rights described in this report
(including CCPA and GDPR) may not apply to you or to all data sources
identified. TraceBurn provides general information about legal frameworks and
does not provide legal advice.
```

### Scraping and Data Collection Disclaimer

```
DATA COLLECTION: TraceBurn accesses only publicly available information and
respects robots.txt directives and rate limits. TraceBurn does not access
authenticated or gated data. Scans are performed solely on behalf of the
authenticated user for the purpose of identifying their own personal data
exposure.
```

### Full Terms of Service Summary Clause (for TraceBurn ToS)

```
PERMITTED USE: TraceBurn may only be used to scan for your own personal
information or for personal information of individuals who have given you
explicit written authorization to act on their behalf (e.g., a family member
or legal dependent). Using TraceBurn to scan for information about other
individuals without authorization may violate applicable law and is prohibited
by these Terms.

TraceBurn is not a data broker, investigative service, or background check
service. It is a personal privacy tool intended to help individuals understand
and reduce their own data exposure.
```

## Summary

| Framework | Jurisdiction | Max Response Time | Enforceable via | Best For |
|-----------|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|----------|
| CCPA/CPRA | California residents | 45 days (90 max) | CA AG, CPPA | US data broker removal |
| GDPR Art. 17 | EU residents / EU-operated orgs | 30 days (90 max) | National DPAs | EU company data removal |
| DMCA | US | Variable | Federal courts | Copyrighted content on paste sites |
| hiQ / CFAA | US (9th Cir.) | N/A | Legal precedent | Public scraping permissibility |

**Key takeaway for TraceBurn:** CCPA is the primary legal lever for US users targeting data brokers. GDPR applies to EU users and can be invoked against major tech platforms globally. Breach data and public records are effectively non-removable — mitigation and monitoring are the correct response. All scraping activity must be restricted to publicly accessible data with appropriate rate limiting and User-Agent disclosure.
